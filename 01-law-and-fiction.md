### *The Tool Strand — Paraphrasis, or How to Translate a Name Back Into the Thing It Claims to Be*

*The Tool Strand — Paraphrasis, or How to Translate a Name Back Into the Thing It Claims to Be*

The Tool Strand — Paraphrasis, or How to Translate a Name Back Into the Thing It Claims to Be

This is the strand that hands the rest of the project its instrument. Every other strand runs an argument on a particular kind of object — a printing plate, a song catalogue, an auction hammer, a school credential, a switch on a wire, a wager at a window. This strand is about the move you make in your head before any of those arguments will land: the discipline of taking a name and translating it back into the real entities and the real transactions the name claims to describe, then asking whether the name still survives the translation. The lawyers have a word for the move; the working-class grammar has another; and the whole long contest of the project is between people who run the translation and people who depend on the translation never being run.

### The Lawyer's Word

The English jurist Jeremy Bentham, writing through the late eighteenth and early nineteenth centuries, gave the move its formal name: paraphrasis. The technique is straightforward and brutal. When the world hands you a sentence containing a fictitious entity — the corporation, the trust, the right, the obligation, the limited edition, the brand, the soul of a partnership, the will of a people — you do not engage the sentence on its own terms. You rewrite it so that every fictitious entity is replaced by the real entities and the real transactions it purports to describe, and then you see whether the rewritten sentence is true. If it is, the fiction was doing honest work — a shorthand for a real arrangement among real people. If the rewrite collapses or comes out absurd, the fiction was doing something else: hiding an empty claim, smuggling authority, or pricing nothing as something.

Bentham's quarrel was with the lawyers and the moralists of his day who used fictitious entities as if they were real things — "the rights of man," "the will of the legislature," "the duty owed to the Crown" — and proceeded as if you could reason about them the way you reason about a chair or a debt. He thought this was nonsense, and he thought it was dangerous nonsense, because the man who controls the fiction controls the people who agree to be reasoned about in its terms. Translate "the will of the Crown" back into real entities and you get a particular living monarch's particular living decisions, with all the history and competence and corruption that any particular person comes with. Leave the fiction in place and you get something that cannot be argued with, because nothing inside the sentence corresponds to any actual person or transaction the argument could reach.

The technique survived Bentham. C. K. Ogden, the linguist and editor who brought Bentham's theory of fictions to a wider audience in the 1930s, sharpened the move into a general intellectual hygiene: the discipline of translating any abstract term into its concrete referents before letting it govern your reasoning. Hans Vaihinger, the German philosopher, ran the same move from the other direction in *The Philosophy of As-If*, arguing that the productive fictions of human thought — the *as-ifs* by which mathematics, law, and morality operate — work precisely because we know they are fictions and use them deliberately, and become destructive only when we forget. The whole twentieth-century critique of ideology — the trick of asking, beneath the noble word, *whose interest is this serving* — is paraphrasis in another costume.

The technique does not require a law degree. It requires only that you ask, of any name that wants to govern your reasoning, the simple question: translate it back into the real entities and the real transactions, and see if the sentence still holds.

### The Grocer's Grammar

My father did not know the word paraphrasis. He knew the move. He ran a grocery for working people in Pittsburgh and he ran it on the principle that every sentence written on a label, every promise made by a salesman, every claim wrapped in a higher word, had to be translated back into what was actually in the crate and what was actually being asked of you in the trade. *Premium grade.* Translate it: what does the supplier mean by premium, what is in the crate, what does it cost me wholesale, what can I sell it for, who pays the difference. *Family pack.* Translate it: how many ounces, what is the per-ounce price compared to the regular pack, is the buyer paying for the cardboard. *Limited time only.* Translate it: how long is the actual window, is the discount real against the recent price or against an inflated reference price, will the same offer reappear next month.

He did not have contempt for the language. He had a working man's respect for what language is for, which is to carry information accurately between people who are about to make a trade. He had contempt only for language that hid the trade — the prestige word over the working word, the higher noun over the simpler one, the certificate over the thing. He taught me to read past the certificate to the thing. He taught me, in fact, paraphrasis, in the kitchen and at the counter and at the cage where he laid his dollar down, decades before I encountered the word in a book. The lawyer's word and the grocer's grammar are the same drill. The lawyer wrote it down. The grocer ran it on every transaction of his working life.

This is the strand's first claim, and it costs nothing to make: the discipline of translating fictitious entities back into real entities is not the property of the academy. It is the property of anyone who has had to trade in a market where the seller has more information than the buyer and uses words to keep it. The grocer runs paraphrasis or he goes broke. The bookmaker runs it or the house loses. The mechanic runs it on the warranty or he replaces the part for free. The discipline is everywhere working people work. The academy gave it a name. The name belongs to them.

### The Move in Six Steps

For anyone who wants the technique in a form they can run on something hard, here it is, broken into the steps Bentham ran implicitly and the grocer ran in his head:

One. Identify the fictitious entity in the sentence. The corporation, the brand, the right, the edition, the credential, the rating, the index, the certificate, the catalog, the chart, the algorithm, the platform, the cloud. Any noun that is doing the load-bearing work without itself being touchable.

Two. List the real entities the fictitious entity claims to summarize. Who are the actual people. What are the actual objects. What are the actual transactions. Who signs, who pays, who delivers, who receives.

Three. Rewrite the sentence with every fictitious entity replaced by its list of real entities and real transactions. The sentence will become longer and uglier. That is the point. The fiction's whole job was to be short and pretty.

Four. Read the rewritten sentence and ask: is this still true? Is the trade described actually happening? Is the authority claimed actually held by someone? Is the promise made actually backed by someone willing to deliver?

Five. If the sentence holds, the fiction was doing honest work. Use it again next time, knowing what it stands for. If the sentence collapses, the fiction was doing something else. Name what it was doing — hiding a transaction, smuggling an authority, pricing nothing as something, conferring a right without a duty — and then walk away from the sentence with your eyes open.

Six. When you act in the world, act on the translation, not on the fiction. Buy the crate, not the label. Read the production record, not the marketing name. Test the door the keeper claims is locked. Lay your dollar against the dream knowing it is a dollar against a dream.

That is the whole technique. The rest of this project is six fronts on which the technique gets run.

### What the Technique Has Always Been For

The technique is old because the problem is old. The first scribe who wrote down a king's claim and discovered, on rereading it, that the claim could not be translated into anything the people would actually do — that scribe was running paraphrasis. The Roman jurist who distinguished the legal person from the natural person and noted, carefully, that the legal person had no body to be punished — running paraphrasis. The medieval theologian who asked whether the soul was substance or relation — running paraphrasis. The American framers who wrote *We the People* knowing perfectly well that *the people* could not sign a document and that some particular living men were doing the signing — running paraphrasis, and running it in the open. The fiction admitted as fiction is the productive kind. The fiction insisted on as fact is the kind paraphrasis is built to dissolve.

The slow war this project tracks is, at its root, a war over whether the fictions get to be insisted on as facts. The corporation gets to be a person — for some purposes, in some courts, with some immunities the natural person does not have. The brand gets to be a maker — even after the maker is dead, even when no maker was ever named, even when the brand is a placeholder over a corporate filing whose actual signatory is a different person. The credential gets to be the knowing — even when the credential was earned through a process that did not produce the knowing. The chart gets to be the canon — even when the chart was assembled by the same parties whose inventory the chart's assertions support. The fiction wants to be the fact. The discipline of paraphrasis is what stands between the wanting and the having.

### The Refusal to Run It

Here is the harder thing to say, and the strand is the place to say it. The technique is simple. Most people do not run it. They do not run it because running it is work, because the fictions are usually flattering and the translations are usually unflattering, because the keepers of the fictions are organized and the runners of the translation are not, and because — Dostoevsky knew this and the capstone will name it — most people, offered the burden of holding their own book, ask to be relieved of it. The fiction relieves them. The translation makes them responsible for what the translation reveals. The technique is free. The cost is what you have to do once you have run it.

This is why the technique falls to particular people. The lawyer running paraphrasis on a contract because the contract is what he has been hired to read against the world. The grocer running it on the wholesale invoice because the margin will not survive otherwise. The reporter running it on the press release because the press release was written by someone whose interest is not the reporter's. The activist running it on the official statement because the official statement was the cover story for the operation the activist is trying to expose. The artist running it on the catalog raisonné because the catalog is being used to ratify a fiction the artist's own production record refuses. The tester runs the translation because the tester is the person for whom the gap between the fiction and the world is costly.

And the felicitous version of the technique, which the capstone will name as the project's answer, is the version where the testing is distributed across many hands. A culture in which paraphrasis is normal — taught in schools, practiced in markets, expected of journalists, demanded of officials — is a culture in which no single fiction can grow large enough to do real damage before someone runs the translation. The opposite culture, in which paraphrasis is rare and the fictions are protected, is the culture in which the gap between the book and the world is allowed to grow until it snaps shut indiscriminately on everyone underneath it. The technique is the immune system. A culture that abandons it gets sick.

### How This Strand Hands Off to the Others

The Industry strand runs paraphrasis on the printing plate. Translate "an authentic Hirst, made in year 145, dated 1991" back into real entities and you get: an object made by a buyer from an instruction, signed by an heir, falsely dated, sold under the name of a man a century and a half in his grave. The sentence does not survive. The fiction was carrying value the production record could not carry, and the legal academy lands on trademark — the perpetual source-identifier — as the only structure that survives the translation. The Industry strand documents the move. The Law strand named the technique that catches it.

The Media strand runs paraphrasis on the catalogue and the news cycle. Translate "the press is reporting" back into real entities and you get particular outlets with particular advertising relationships writing particular pieces under particular editorial pressures, and you discover that the coverage is, in many cases, an advertising function wearing news's clothes. The strand documents the architecture; the technique catches the architecture every time the strand applies it.

The Markets strand runs paraphrasis on the auction hammer and the chart. Translate "edition of 25, 2003, acquired from Lazarides Gallery" back into real entities and you get a gallery that was not incorporated until 2004, a publisher not incorporated until 2004, an authentication body not incorporated until 2008, and a chart assembled in 2021 by parties with an interest in the verification. The sentence does not survive. The Markets strand documents the production record; the technique catches the gap.

The Education strand runs paraphrasis on the credential. Translate "a degree from this institution" back into real entities and you get a particular faculty teaching a particular curriculum to a particular student over a particular period, and the question becomes whether the production record matches what the credential claims. The strand documents the renaming of academies as universities, the credential as class migration; the technique catches the move every time.

The e-train strand runs paraphrasis on the wire, the cloud, the switch, the register. Translate "the cloud" back into real entities and you get a data center, a building with cooling and power and physical disks owned by a corporation in a jurisdiction with particular laws. The fiction of dematerialized infrastructure dissolves the moment the translation is run. The strand documents the materiality the fiction was built to obscure.

And the Grand Game capstone runs paraphrasis on the wager itself — on the book that records every hand, the door that may or may not be locked, the keeper whose authority may or may not be real, the medicine show whose elixir may or may not work. The capstone is what paraphrasis looks like applied to the whole of culture, run continuously, by ordinary people, on the fictions that govern their lives.

### The Tool

So this is the strand's only claim, and it is the claim every other strand depends on. There is a technique. It is old, it is named, it is simple, and it is the difference between a culture that catches its gap between fiction and world while the gap is still small and a culture that lets the gap grow until it snaps shut. The technique is paraphrasis in the academy and shit-from-shinola at the counter and *read the picture, not the caption* in the working-class grammar this project has tried to honor throughout. It is the same technique under three names. Bentham wrote it down. The grocer ran it. The project asks you to run it on whatever fiction is currently being insisted on as fact in your own life.

The remaining strands are the demonstration. Each takes a particular kind of fictitious entity — plate, catalogue, hammer, credential, switch, wager — and runs the translation. The technique is the tool. The strands are the work. The capstone is the wager the work was always about.

Read the picture, not the caption.
