# The Hammers Are In, the Press Calls It a Win, and the Math Says Otherwise
### A post-Wednesday follow-up to *Banksy's Witching Hour*. Working class. Plain English. The trade words go in parentheses so they translate as they go.

---

A week ago I wrote a piece called *Banksy's Witching Hour* that said two paintings hammering in New York on Wednesday night were a test — not of taste, but of whether a whole inventory class held its price. Same image (a *Girl and Balloon*), same evening, two hours apart, twenty-fold spread between the two estimates. That was the test. The hammers are in. The press is reporting a success. The numbers, set next to each other, say something else, and that's what this piece is about.

I am writing this for the people who love art as a thing they live with *and* as a thing they spent money on — both at once, no shame in either, because that's what most of us are. I'm going to use the trade's language only when I have to, and when I do I'll put what it actually means right next to it in parentheses, because the trade's whole hustle, four years of looking at it now, is to make plain things sound special so the price tag survives the translation.

## What actually hammered

Wednesday, 20 May 2026, Christie's New York, Lot 10A. *Girl and Balloon*, sixteen inches by sixteen, spray on canvas, tagged 'BANKSY' on the edge, numbered '10/25 2003' on the stretcher (the wood frame the canvas is stapled to). The catalog says it was acquired from Lazarides Gallery, London, in 2003. **Sold for $953,000 including premium** (the buyer's fee on top of the hammer).

Now set that next to the head-to-head match. Eighteen months earlier, September 2024, Christie's Hong Kong, *Girl and Balloon*, same exact thing — sixteen by sixteen, spray on canvas, stenciled BANKSY on the side, this one numbered 21/25, also dated 2003. **Hammered HKD 8,568,000, about USD 1,100,430.**

Two prints from the same stated edition of twenty-five (the *edition* is the run; numbered out of the total). Same year, same image, same size, same maker's mark. One a year and a half ago at $1.1 million. The other Wednesday at $953,000. **Down about thirteen percent on a head-to-head match.** That's not a market passing a test. That's the price softening on the exact same object in the exact same room, eighteen months apart, while every other asset class on earth went up. The chart (the website that catalogs which prints are real) and the press are calling it a clean clear. The two hammers, side by side, are calling it a slow leak.

## And then there was Hirst

Same evening, same house, Lot 13A. Damien Hirst, *Au-195m*, a big spot painting (the dots-on-a-grid series the studio has been turning out for decades), seventy-six by ninety-two inches, painted 2008, provenance White Cube → Christie's 2012 → present owner. Estimate $600,000 to $800,000.

**Hammered $450,000.**

That's not "near the low." That's a quarter below the low estimate, on a flagship-tier work, on the same Wednesday the Banksy was supposed to be the proof that the brand-as-asset-class architecture (treating an artist's name like a stock) was still holding. Banksy slipped on a head-to-head match. Hirst couldn't make his floor. Both numbers tell the same story: the people who paid 2018–2022 peaks for these names are sitting on positions worth less than the marks, and the secondary market (the resale market after the first buyer, where everyone in the world prices their copy off the latest public hammer) just told them so. Out loud. In front of the press, which is currently writing it up as a success.

## What the chart calls things, and what they actually are

Here is the part that is shit-from-shinola 101 (the country expression my dad would have used: you have to be able to tell one from the other or you're going to have a bad day). The Banksy Explained website — the unofficial catalog (a "catalog raisonné" is the master list of an artist's real work) — has two categories for the prints in question. One is **prints on paper**. The other is **prints on canvas**, and on the website that second category is labeled *Editioned Canvases*.

Read those two names back to back. *Prints on paper* and *Editioned canvases*. Notice what's missing from the second one. The word **print**. Dropped. Quietly. As if the substrate (the surface the picture lives on) is the thing that turns one into the other. As if putting the same stencil with the same few layers on canvas instead of paper Rumpelstiltskins (the fairy tale: straw spun into gold overnight by a man whose name nobody remembers) the same object into a different category of thing. A canvas costs more than a sheet of paper. The production method is identical. The stencil is the same stencil. What changes is the substrate and the name on the line in the catalog.

That matters because **the prints on paper already repriced**. Phillips London, January 2026, repriced the prints-on-paper category 75 to 90 percent below the 2021 peaks. The hammer on an unsigned *Girl with Balloon* edition of six hundred at Christie's London on 31 March 2026 was £88,900. The peak was £2 million. The chart's lower tier has already given the answer the market gave it. The *upper* tier — the **prints on canvas** the chart calls "Editioned Canvases" — has been held up by an informal hold pact (the holders who own the inventory agreeing not to dump it at prices that would force everyone else to mark down too). Wednesday was the first head-to-head public test of whether that upper tier was going to follow the paper tier down. The head-to-head match says yes, it's started, and the Hirst flagship missing its low says the cascade isn't staying in the Banksy lane either.

## The gallery that didn't exist

There is one documentary fact about Wednesday's Banksy lot that no buyer should have signed a check without checking. The provenance (the chain of who-owned-it, the paper trail) on Lot 10A says: *Lazarides Gallery, London. Acquired from the above by the present owner in 2003.*

**Lazarides Gallery did not legally exist in 2003.** Lazarides Limited was incorporated at Companies House (the UK government register where every limited company has to file) on 2 April 2004 as a shell called Finlaw 457, and renamed Lazarides Limited on 19 August 2004. Pictures on Walls, the publisher that supposedly produced this run, was incorporated 2 June 2004. The Pest Control Office, the authentication body that issued the certificate of authenticity attached to Wednesday's lot, was not incorporated until 2008.

None of the three entities that anchor this canvas's identity as "edition of 25, 2003, from Lazarides Gallery" existed as legal entities when the canvas was supposedly produced and sold. The dealer named on the provenance line, Steve Lazarides himself, said in his own 2019 book *Banksy Captured* that the *Girl and Balloon* stencil first appeared on canvas at the December 2004 Santa's Ghetto show. **Not 2003.** The man on the receipt contradicted the receipt's date five years before this auction, in his own published account.

The chart that ratifies the provenance was assembled in 2021. It catalogs canvas editions from 2000 through 2005, then stops. The 2006 *Love Is In The Air* canvas the maker signed **16/15** on the back — sixteen out of fifteen, which is not an edition number, which is a notation a person makes when they are saying *this is the boundary, the run is over, what comes after is not mine* — is not on the chart. The chart was built downstream of the very claims it is being used to verify, by the very operators who would benefit from the verification, in the year the venture's corporate close-out completed.

## What's actually in those boxes

Here is the part that is hard to read past once you see it, and your eyes can do the reading without any expert. The original 2002 *Girl and Balloon* canvases that appear in the earliest auction records are small — six inches by six inches. Studio-scale. Test-print scale. The size a person prints when they are working something out. The canvases now showing up at Christie's at the major-house pegging level (the public-benchmark-setting moment that determines what every comparable holder of the same inventory marks their copy at) are sixteen by sixteen, with a provenance line that says 2003 and an *acquired from a gallery that wasn't incorporated yet*.

What appears to have happened — read the public auction records, the published dealer's account, the Companies House dates, and you can put it together yourself without me — is that a small early run at studio size got expanded retrospectively into a larger, marketable format, with the larger format's provenance backdated to a year the gallery couldn't have sold it in, and the chart ratifying the backdate so the bigger canvases would have papers to sell against. The bigger canvases were populated by the dealer later. The papers were written to match. The chart was assembled to ratify. The market was sold the result.

The 16/15 notation in 2006 is what a maker does when she will not sign the next round of that. The press has spent twenty years not reading the back of that canvas.

## The press call vs. the math

So: Hong Kong September 2024, an edition-mate of Wednesday's lot, **$1.1 million.** New York Wednesday May 2026, the head-to-head match on a different number from the same stated edition, **$953,000.** Down thirteen percent in eighteen months on a head-to-head edition-mate while the rest of the world's asset prices climbed. Hirst flagship same night, **$450,000 against a $600K low** — a *quarter under the floor*. The press is calling it Banksy's market passing its first big test since the identity coverage. Read the picture, not the caption (the rule my dad taught me at the cage where he laid down a dollar most days and didn't pretend a dollar was a fortune): the *picture* is two hammers that softened against their comparables. The *caption* is "success." The picture and the caption are saying different things.

This is the part of the trade where I would put **secondary-market mark-to-market cascade** in the sentence if I were trying to sound like the trade. So here it is, plain: every holder of comparable inventory anywhere in the world — Dubai, Hong Kong, Moscow, Shanghai, Singapore, the family offices, the sovereign-wealth-adjacent buyers, the celebrity collectors who got sold in at peak — just had the price they could reasonably expect to get for their own copy revised downward, in public, by these two hammers. They don't have to sell to take the markdown. The mark is the price the latest public sale set. The leverage placed against the inventory — the loans, the credit lines, the family-office allocations, the cultural-asset baskets some of these holdings ride inside — comes due at the new lower marks. The cascade is not the auction. The cascade is what *every other holder of comparable inventory has to do tomorrow morning* because of the auction.

## Editioned canvases as the beanie babies of the eight-year financialization

A few years back, somebody listed about 900,000 Beanie Babies on eBay. Plush toys. The 1990s craze, the one where people went to McDonald's not for hamburgers but for the toy, where the secondary market (resale, again) priced individual bears at thousands of dollars, where people kept them in plastic cases as a *store of value* (a thing you buy not to use but to hold because you think it will be worth more later). That was the bet. The bet was that the scarcity claimed by the manufacturer (Ty Warner, the man who made them) was real and durable. Nine hundred thousand listings on eBay told the world the scarcity was a story. Ty Warner cashed in. Approximately nobody else did.

The chart of "Editioned Canvases" is the gold-foil sticker on the bottom of a beanie baby. It says *limited edition of twenty-five*. The original production was a small set of six-by-sixes. The market was sold sixteen-by-sixteens with backdated provenance from a gallery that wasn't incorporated. Somewhere in the hundreds — call it three to four hundred individual objects — sit in the inventory at *book value* (what the holder carries them at for accounting and lending purposes) of around three hundred thousand to four hundred thousand dollars each. They have not, most of them, been to a major public auction in years. Wednesday's hammer just told them what they're worth in the open. The chart told them they were limited edition fine art. The hammer told them they were canvas with stencil on it, two of the same number softening thirteen percent in eighteen months, while the flagship of the brand-they-rode-with missed its floor by a quarter.

The trade calls this the **pegging event** (the public benchmark that sets the value of every comparable holding the inventory holders still have on their books). The grocer's son calls it Wednesday told the truth.

## The structural question the next post answers

There is one more thing to put on the record, and it's the question the whole four-year project was built to put plainly. The corporate filings — at Companies House and at the Jersey Financial Services Commission (the offshore register where the parent holding company sits) — show **Damien Hirst as the Artist of Record** (the legally named principal on the founding shells: Turtleneck, Pictures on Walls, the broader vehicles), through the documented period of the venture. *Artist of Record* is a role: the corporate-signatory, the financier, the legal principal on the paper. It is not the same role as **the maker** — the hand that did the original conceiving and executing. Those are two different functions, and the chart that the auction catalogs lean on conflates them, because the conflation is what lets the editions get priced at painting-tier numbers.

The 16/15 notation in 2006 is what it looks like when the maker says: *the venture continues without me past this date.* The escalation of the brand for twenty years after 2006 is what it looks like when the Artist of Record keeps the run going without the maker. Two different roles, two different functions, twenty years apart in the operation's life — and the chart, and the press, treated them as one person the whole time.

That's the structural question. I am not naming the maker in this post, because the maker has the right to that privacy and the structure is the point. I am naming the Artist of Record because he is named on the public corporate filings, openly, and has been since 1997. He is the one the receipts are made out to.
