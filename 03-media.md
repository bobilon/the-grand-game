# Media

### *The Voice That Learned to Sell*

*“In the beginning was the Word, and the Word was with God, and the Word was God.”*

> *— The Gospel of John 1:1 (Authorized Version, 1611)*

*“And was Jerusalem builded here / Among these dark Satanic Mills?”*

— William Blake, c. 1809

### Prologue: The Word Before the Page

Begin four thousand years back, because the thing this strand follows is older than any of its instruments, and the shape it takes at the end is only legible against where it started. The first author of the first book could not speak. Moses, given the Law, protests in Exodus that he is slow of speech and of a slow tongue — a stammer, the tradition says, a lisp — and God appoints his brother Aaron to be his mouth. So the founding scene of scripture is a maker who holds the Word but cannot voice it, who needs another’s mouth to deliver what was given to him, and who in any case does not own it: the Word belongs to the authority above him; he only carries it down the mountain.

And the name revealed to him there is itself an impossibility — not a linguistically intelligible noun that can be parsed, cataloged, or traded, but a breathing syntax closer to the soul of a breeze, a vocalized inhalation (YHWH) that cannot be spoken without merely breathing. It is pure, uncopyable animation. Yet it begins oral. The Law is spoken, handed down, memorized, carried in the body before it is ever fixed in a text — a tradition that, for a long time, did not want to be a book at all.

There was a reason to fear the book, and the Greeks named it. In Plato’s Phaedrus, around 370 BCE, Socrates tells the Egyptian myth of Theuth, the god who invents letters and presents them to King Thamus as an elixir of memory and wisdom. Thamus refuses the gift: writing, he says, will produce forgetfulness in those who learn it, because they will trust the external marks instead of remembering from within, and it will give them the appearance of wisdom without the reality. The written word cannot answer back; it cannot adapt to the reader the way living speech can; it is, Plato says, a pharmakon — remedy and poison at once. This is the oldest anxiety about the climb this strand traces: that fixing the word kills something living in it, that the page is where the voice goes to die. Twenty-three centuries later a French philosopher would say nearly the same thing in different words, and a Magnum photographer would quit the press over a version of it. The fear is the through-line. Hold it.

Two more markers, and then the climb proper. Babel is the story of language as the conspiracy against heaven — one tongue, one people, a tower rising, and the divine response is not to topple the tower but to scatter the language, to break the one speech into the many so that the builders can no longer understand one another. The Word unified is a threat; the Word divided is safe. And then the Word travels: the Septuagint, the Hebrew scriptures rendered into Greek in the third and second centuries BCE, carries the book out of one people’s tongue and into the imperial language of the Mediterranean — the road that runs from Greek toward Latin, toward the vernaculars, toward English. The Hebrew canon closes; the book is finished; and the Word, now portable across languages, begins its long journey toward the printing press fourteen centuries ahead.

And at the midpoint of that journey stands the line at the top of this page. The Gospel of John — conventionally dated to the end of the first century, though a serious minority argues earlier — opens not with a birth but with a metaphysics: In the beginning was the Word, and the Word was with God, and the Word was God. Read the turn in it, because it is the turn the whole strand will industrialize. The Word was with the authority — and then the Word was the authority. Before John, the Word is an ungraspable breath or a Law handed down, owned from above, carried by a stammering scribe. After John, the Word is the thing itself, incarnate, the name that is the presence. That is the hinge: the moment the Word stops pointing at the maker and becomes the maker. From here the rest of this strand is that single verse, dragged through fourteen hundred years to Gutenberg and then four hundred more to the auction block, and monetized: the Word was with the maker, then the Word was the maker, and then — at the very end — the maker vanished into the Word and the Word was sold.

One more image at the front, because it is the fire that bookends everything. At Alexandria stood the great Library, the Mouseion, the temple of the Muses — the gathered written word of the ancient world in one place. Its burning is the West’s founding myth of lost knowledge, and it is worth being honest that it is a myth: there was no single catastrophic fire; the Library declined over centuries through many smaller losses. But the myth is the truth the culture chose to keep — the story of the word gathered and then consumed, the fragility of everything written when it sits in one place. Remember the shape of it. At the very end of this strand we will reach the next Alexandria, and discover that it does not burn. It is locked.

### Opening: The Fire and the Elevator

Blake wrote his dark Satanic Mills around 1809, and it was a question he had to ask by the fix he saw coming down the track, looking at the gnashing looms of England’s newborn textile industry grinding down the artisanal tradition with the extreme prejudice of a horde. He knew why the caged bird beats its wing till its blood is red on the cruel bars. Jerusalem as accusation and incantation — a discontent that floats forward a generation, crosses an ocean, and heads toward the American sequel taking shape in a river valley poised to catch flame.

By the 1840s, Pittsburgh was a city built of timber, packed along the point where the Monongahela meets the Allegheny to form the Ohio — and on the tenth of April, 1845, it burned. The Great Fire took roughly a third of the city in eighteen hours: some sixty acres, twelve hundred buildings, twelve thousand people left homeless, the wooden Monongahela Bridge reduced to ash at the river’s edge. Two people died. And then the thing that tells you what kind of place this was: the city did not mourn the fire so much as feed on it. Property values soared, the demand for brick and stone revived the industrial base, the construction boom began four days later, and the timber town came back harder — reforged in stone and iron, a fire the emerging order converted directly into momentum. For a century afterward, Pittsburgh measured its history by that blaze: the before and the after. The Satanic Mills were no longer a metaphor. They were an address, and the address had just burned and been rebuilt to burn hotter. This is the second fire of the strand; the Library was the first; hold them both.

Out of that rebuilt valley came two things at once: the steel that would build the country, and the modern corporate person that would own the steel. Andrew Carnegie is the keystone, and the crucial fact about him is that he came up on the railroad before he ever touched a furnace. A poor Scottish immigrant, he started at fourteen as a telegraph messenger, caught the eye of Thomas Scott of the Pennsylvania Railroad, became Scott’s private telegrapher in 1853, and by 1859 had succeeded him as superintendent of the railroad’s Pittsburgh division. The railroad was his university, and its lesson was the one he built an empire on: control the bottlenecks — the rails, the bridges, the ore. From the train he could see the whole supply chain at once and assembled them into the first great vertically integrated plant, buying the coke fields and the iron-ore deposits and the ships and the railroads that fed the mills, so nothing he needed could be withheld from him. When he opened his first steel works in 1875 he named it for J. Edgar Thomson, president of the Pennsylvania Railroad, and promptly received an enormous rail order from that railroad. The brand-name as a business instrument, performed in steel.

The empire’s terminus is the corporate-person Goliath. In 1901 Carnegie sold to J. P. Morgan for four hundred and eighty million dollars, and the sale created U.S. Steel, the first billion-dollar corporation. Around it the same valley seeded the named entities that would dominate the century — Gulf Oil, Alcoa, the Mellon Bank, Westinghouse, Heinz — the first nationally branded corporate persons, born where the city had burned. And Carnegie himself was the first celebrity brand among them: “the Richest Man in the World,” a public identity he authored on purpose, down to writing The Gospel of Wealth to compose his own legend. He is the prototype of the named human sold as a brand, decades before Hearst, a century before Banksy. Hold one detail, because it rhymes the whole way down: the Pittsburgh fortunes kept having their revelations on trains. Carnegie saw his supply chain from a railroad car. H. J. Heinz, by his own account, conceived his immortal slogan while riding a New York elevated train and spotting an ad for a shoe store boasting “21 Styles.” He thought: we do not have styles, we have varieties — counted his products, found well past sixty, and chose “57” anyway, five being his lucky number and seven his wife’s. There were never fifty-seven varieties. There are hundreds now. The number is a pure fiction, manufactured in 1896 and sold as a fact ever since, and it worked because most manufacturers were then unknown to the people who bought their goods; Heinz made the label the asset, until the name outsold the pickle. That is the whole endgame of this strand, rehearsed on a ketchup bottle. Banksy’s edition of twenty-five and Heinz’s fifty-seven varieties are the same invented number, and both were dreamed up on a train.

Now the other opening, the small human one, because the strand needs both poles from the start. In 1893, in a Dayton department store, a young Black man ran an elevator for four dollars a week. The son of formerly enslaved parents — his father had escaped, then returned to fight in the 55th Massachusetts — he had been the only Black student in his high school, where a classmate named Orville Wright printed his neighborhood newspaper. He could not afford college; Dayton would hire him for nothing better than the elevator. So he wrote poems, borrowed a hundred and twenty-five dollars to print them, and sold the book to his passengers, a dollar a copy, hand to hand, until the loan was repaid. The book was Oak and Ivy. The man was Paul Laurence Dunbar, and he became the first Black American to make a living from his writing. Carnegie owned the mills and the rivers and his own legend. Dunbar owned one thing — his book — and he owned it only because no one had yet thought it worth taking. Between those two figures, the magnate who branded himself and the poet who hand-sold his own asset in an elevator, runs everything that follows.

### The slow war

Set down the frame plainly, because it governs the rest. There is a kind of war that is not an event but a condition — not a battle with a start and an end, but the standing state underneath, the thing that was always already there, that peace only ever interrupts. It does not announce itself. It simmers. Call it the slow war. In this strand it is the permanent low contention over a single question: when a made thing becomes valuable, who owns it — the one who made it, or the one who holds the paper? And it is older than any of its modern costumes, because it is finally a question of who holds the Word. The one who names owns; the one who is named is owned. That structure runs the whole length of the four-thousand-year line: Father and child in the church, the authority naming the soul. King or Queen and subject, the crown naming its people. Master and slave, the name written on a body that cannot refuse it. Then Management and Labor at the mill gate, and Artist and Corporation at the contract table. Different clothes; one war; one structure underneath — the maker makes, the holder owns, and the value the maker created flows to the paper, not the hand. The reader will usually know which pairing is live. Homestead fought it with guns. Foster fought it with thirty-eight cents in his pocket. It is the ground the whole climb stands on.

Because here is the climb itself, in one breath. The word rose off the page — into the serial, the song, the moving picture, the broadcast — and the whole way up it learned that the thing which keeps earning is the thing that wants to be copied. The book business learned this through copyright a century before the art-brand operators bet the other way, on the static trophy that must not move. The media business learned it in public, at industrial scale, by lifting up the people America had locked out — the immigrant, the Jew, the Black American — and by perfecting, along the way, the most sophisticated lie a corporation ever told: that a fiction could be sold as a living hand. The climb is the escalation. The slow war is the undercurrent. And the fire runs through both, from the Library to the end: Alexandria’s fade, the 1845 reforging, and — still ahead — the fire next time, and the flare aimed at the builders.

### Movement I — The Word as a Daily Asset

Before the song and the screen, the first mass medium was the printed installment — the newspaper and magazine serial — and the literature we now teach as canon was, in its own time, popular serial product. Dickens ended chapters on cliffhangers precisely to sell the next number; readers mobbed the docks for the installment that would reveal whether Little Nell lived. And the Americans lived in the periodical as fully as the British. Mark Twain and Edgar Allan Poe were magazine men, writing to the rhythm of the issue, and Poe in particular built the modern machinery of the detective and horror tale as serial commodity — then died chasing the dream of owning his own magazine. That dream is the slow war in embryo: the maker who understands that the real asset is not the story but the channel, and who tries, and fails, to own it. Harriet Beecher Stowe’s Uncle Tom’s Cabin ran as a serial before it was a book — the installment as political weapon, prying open the national conscience one issue at a time. The serial is the word as a daily and weekly asset, the thing that earns by making you come back, and the installment that wants to be continued is the direct ancestor of the character that wants to be redrawn.

### Movement II — The Word as a Song

Stephen Foster: the slow war’s first casualty

The slow war has a first casualty in this strand, and he is a Pittsburgh man. Stephen Foster, born in Lawrenceville in 1826, was the first person in America to try to make a living by selling his songs — and the contracts he wrote out in his own hand are the earliest known agreements between an American songwriter and a music publisher. He was inventing the songwriter’s royalty before there was any business to invent it in: no recordings, no radio, no performing-rights societies, nothing but the sale of sheet music. And his ending is this entire strand’s thesis written in one body. His songs swept the country — “Oh! Susanna” became the anthem of the Gold Rush — and were pirated by more than two dozen publishing houses that earned tens of thousands while Foster, across his whole life, earned about fifteen thousand dollars in royalties and almost nothing in performance. He died at thirty-seven in a New York hotel with thirty-eight cents in his pocket and a scrap of paper that read dear friends and gentle hearts. The man who invented the songwriter’s contract died broke while the copies made everyone else rich. That is the slow war’s first casualty — Artist and Corporation, before the corporation even had a name for itself — and the wound he opens runs forward to every maker who fought to own the thing they made. The poet in the elevator owned his book because no one wanted it yet. Foster wanted to be paid for the copy at the very moment the copy was becoming the asset, and it killed him.

### Tin Pan Alley: the note becomes an asset

If the printed word became property through copyright, the sung word was industrialized on Tin Pan Alley — the clatter of cheap upright pianos in the West Twenty-Eighth Street block of Manhattan where, from the 1890s into the 1930s, the American song was manufactured. Publishers kept composers and lyricists on salary, ran songs through pluggers who performed them in every vaudeville house and five-and-dime in reach, and sold the result as sheet music by the millions. The song was the purest living asset yet devised: one composition, infinitely performable, earning a royalty every time anyone anywhere played it — written once and paying forever. And once again the builders were the excluded: the children of Jewish immigrants shut out of the established trades, who walked into a business so new it had no door to lock. Irving Berlin arrived from Russia, could barely notate music, and wrote some of the century’s most durable songs by humming them to a transcriber. The pattern that runs through this whole strand announces itself here — the people pushed to the margin built the machine that would carry the culture — and the song was its lightest version, the asset you could fold into an envelope.

### Rodgers, Hammerstein, and the Brill Building: the catalogue and the cubicle

The Alley sold single songs; the next move bound them into structures that could be revived forever. Richard Rodgers spans the evolution in one career: with Lorenz Hart through the 1920s and ’30s, the witty melancholy standard; then with Oscar Hammerstein II, from Oklahoma! in 1943, the integrated musical, where song and story lock so the score cannot be pried loose. That integration was asset engineering as much as art — a bound score is a property licensable to every regional theatre and high school in the country, in perpetuity. Oklahoma!, South Pacific, The Sound of Music: not just shows but annuities. And the Alley’s assembly line did not die; it modernized, a few blocks uptown, in the Brill Building and its less glamorous neighbor at 1650 Broadway, where in the late 1950s and early ’60s the publisher Don Kirshner ran a song factory the New York Times later called an Art Deco sweatshop of smash hits. Carole King and Gerry Goffin, Barry Mann and Cynthia Weil, Jeff Barry and Ellie Greenwich, Neil Sedaka — mostly young working-class Jewish strivers from Brooklyn, raised on the radio singing Berlin and Porter — wrote in cubicles barely big enough for an upright piano and a bench, Kirshner playing one team against the next. It was Tin Pan Alley with a backbeat: the same factory, one generation on, the catalogue still the asset and the writer still in a cubby.

### The appropriation, the cover, and the assembly line that fought back

But the Brill factory ran on a fuel it did not own, and this is where the slow war shows its oldest face. The form was rhythm and blues — Black music — and the machine’s biggest hits were written by white songwriters for Black voices and sold to a mainstream that would not have bought the source directly. The tell is the breakthrough itself: the Shirelles, four Black teenagers, scored the first girl-group number one with “Will You Love Me Tomorrow,” written by Goffin and King. The writers were white; the form was Black; the voices were Black; and the ownership — the publishing, the masters, the machine — belonged to none of the people who made or sang it. That is the slow war at its most exact: the most underpaid labor in the strand is Black, and a fortune in catalogue got built on it.

The cover record was the cruder version of the same theft. In the 1950s the radio was segregated in fact if not in law — ninety percent of American stations would not play an R&B record by Chuck Berry or Fats Domino or Little Richard — so the labels found a clean-cut white singer to re-record the Black song for the white market. Pat Boone is the emblem: in 1956 he remade Little Richard’s “Tutti Frutti” and sanitized it, softening the lyric and the heat, and his version outsold and out-charted the original on pop radio. It is minstrelsy without the burnt cork — the white performer carrying the Black creation to the paying audience, the creator left in the drawer while the cover sat on the dresser, in Little Richard’s own bitter image. There is one honest complication: because the covers were recognizable derivatives, the original writers drew mechanical royalties, so Fats Domino could flash a diamond ring and say Boone’s cover had bought it. The slow war pays the maker a tip and keeps the fortune. The theft was real; the crumb was also real; both are the system.

And then the labor built its own plant. Berry Gordy had worked the Ford assembly line in Detroit, installing chrome and upholstery, and had written songs on the side for a royalty check once reported at three dollars and nineteen cents — the slow war’s wage, exact to the penny. In 1959, with an eight-hundred-dollar family loan, he founded the label that became Motown, and he built it deliberately in the image of the auto plant that had underpaid Black Detroit: a hit factory with its own writers, producers, house band, and finishing school, proud to call itself Detroit’s other world-famous assembly line. Motown became the most successful Black-owned business in America — the form seized, the means of production owned, the crossover that Boone’s covers had been the white workaround for now achieved by the people who made the music. The line runs forward from there: the producer becoming the proprietor, Quincy Jones to Dr. Dre to Jay-Z, the maker becoming the label, the labor becoming the management. It is the one front of the slow war that the people it was waged against can be said to have won — though never cleanly, and never finally: Motown itself now sits inside a global conglomerate, the independent absorbed back into the machine, the victory relocated rather than secured.

And it could not simmer forever without flaring. James Baldwin took the title of The Fire Next Time from an old spiritual — a slave’s coded promise that the next reckoning would come not as Noah’s flood but as fire. That is the slow war’s endpoint when it stops being slow. The appropriated seized their own channel and turned it back on the machine: hip-hop in the late 1980s and early ’90s, Public Enemy’s Burn Hollywood Burn naming the industry’s long theft of Black images and labor outright, the riot-coming register broadcast on the very apparatus that had done the taking. The grievance went electric. The fire Baldwin promised arrived not as flood but as flame, on MTV, in the machine’s own house — the labor indicting the management on management’s own channel. That is the third fire.

### Movement III — The Word and the Image on One Plate

### The newspaper strip and the comic book: the character outlives the maker

The Letters strand made a claim about William Blake — word and image fused on a single plate, etched by the maker’s own hand, the proto-comic. The popular form that descended from it came first in the newspaper, not the comic book: the daily and Sunday strip, the Yellow Kid and Krazy Kat, the word-and-image going to the masses on cheap pulp decades before any superhero. The comic book is that fusion gone industrial — and once again the builders were the locked-out. Jerry Siegel and Joe Shuster, two Jewish teenagers from Cleveland, created Superman and sold the rights for a hundred and thirty dollars — the slow war’s grimmest single transaction, because the character they invented earned fortunes for everyone but them, for decades, while they fought merely to be credited. The character is the living asset in its most extreme form: it outlives its makers and every later hand, accumulating value precisely because it can be drawn again, endlessly. Superman is a master recording you can redraw. And the line runs straight from that basement to the present, where the comic-book character is the most valuable film property on earth. The men who built it were paid in the low hundreds and a lifetime of grievance. The entity that owns it built an empire. The pattern does not change; it only scales.

### The card: the human edition of twenty-five

The most quietly astonishing object in this strand came free in a pack of cigarettes. In the 1880s, manufacturers slipped a stiff blank card into fragile paper packs to keep them from crushing, and someone realized the stiffener could carry a picture. In 1886, Goodwin & Co. issued a series of collectable cards to build brand loyalty, and the trade settled at once on a logic this whole project tracks: cards came in numbered series, usually of twenty-five or fifty on a subject, so the buyer would keep buying until the set was complete. The cigarette card invented the numbered edition of twenty-five — manufactured scarcity to drive repeat purchase — a full century before the art market editioned a single print. Banksy’s edition of twenty-five was waiting in a pack of smokes in 1886. And the card was populist before it was a prize: called “the Working Man’s Encyclopedia,” because working families could not afford books and newspapers carried few pictures, so the cards were how athletes and far-off cities entered an ordinary household. The line runs from cigarette card to baseball card, the human manufactured into a tradable, numbered, scarce object. And it lands where the auction work predicts: the Honus Wagner T206, pulled early because Wagner objected to selling cigarettes, became the holy grail and sold for seven and a quarter million dollars in 2022 — the editioned human brand reverting to a trophy object, exactly as the living-asset thesis says it must when the substrate stops moving. The satirical coda is almost too perfect: in the early 1970s Topps issued Wacky Packages, parody stickers mocking supermarket brands, and for a stretch in 1973–74 they are widely reported to have outsold Topps’ own baseball cards — the spoof of the commodity outselling the commodity it spoofed. (Art Spiegelman, later of Maus, worked on them.) Whether the figure is exact or the trade’s favorite legend, the shape is the Banksy move rehearsed on a bubble-gum sticker: satirize the market from a stall inside the market.

### Movement IV — The Word Climbs Off the Page

### Two germ lines: the studio and the phonograph

The flat word became the moving picture, and two separate industries grew up to carry the climbing word on parallel tracks, from different technologies and different money. They would not merge for decades. The first germ line is film, built by the same kind of people who built the Alley — Eastern European Jewish immigrants locked out of the established industries, who walked into one so new it had no gatekeepers: Laemmle at Universal, Zukor at Paramount, William Fox, Cohn at Columbia, Mayer at MGM, the Warner brothers, Goldwyn. Neal Gabler’s history is blunt about the why — men who had grown up destitute, trying to assimilate where nativism ran hot, and if the corridors of status were closed, the movies offered another way in. And a detail ties them to the slow war’s logic of control: they fled west, in part, to elude Thomas Edison’s Motion Picture Patents Company, the East Coast trust that held an early monopoly on the equipment of film. The excluded outran a patent monopoly to build the living-asset machine of the century — the film catalogue, value compounding by motion. And Gabler’s sharpest line is the doubleness this strand insists on: by creating their idealized America on the screen, they reinvented the country in the image of their fiction; they colonized the American imagination. The excluded built the machine, and the machine manufactured the national dream. You cannot cleanly separate the liberation from the fiction-machine; they are one technology.

The second germ line is the record — and it began not as a content business but as a hardware business. The phonograph was a machine looking for something to play, and the recording industry backed into being a content industry only once the device needed a reason to exist. For decades it ran as its own parallel concern alongside the studios: different origin, different capital, the song-as-recording rather than the song-as-sheet-music. The labels pressed the records, owned the masters, metered the radio — they controlled the copying, and control of the copying was the whole business. Over the century, this parallel line was absorbed: the labels were folded into the studios and the broader media conglomerates, so the two lineages that started apart ended inside the same corporate body. Keep that absorption in view, because it is what sets up the slow war’s bloodiest scene. You can only be made a slave to the master once the entity that owns the master has swallowed you whole.

### The star system, the ringmaster, and the warm face

Inside the studio, the human became a property. The contract player was signed, renamed, taught to walk and speak, given a manufactured biography, and lit until a person became a brand and the name became the tradable thing — the card’s logic at industrial scale, and it ties to the auction work’s endpoint: when the asset is questioned, value retreats to the name. The studios learned that first. And before broadcast, the live spectacle had its master of ceremonies — the circus ringmaster, the P. T. Barnum impresario who manufactured the event and stood at its center selling it. When the signal arrived, that figure simply changed channels: ringmaster to vaudeville host to variety-show emcee to network television. The voice that tells you what you are seeing and how to feel survived the jump intact. Set one warm image against what comes next, because the strand needs its tender pole. In 1979, Coca-Cola ran a commercial in which the Pittsburgh Steelers’ “Mean Joe” Greene, limping exhausted off the field, is offered a Coke by a small boy, drinks it, and tosses the kid his game jersey with a sudden unguarded grin. It became one of the most beloved ads ever made — the corporate fiction at its most affectionate, the Black athlete-as-emblem, the human turned into the warm face of the brand. The corporate persona can genuinely move you; that is exactly why it works. Hearst manufactures fear; Coke manufactures warmth; the technology is the same.

### Hearst: the man who manufactured reality, and the legend that manufactured him

William Randolph Hearst is where the media machine learns it can manufacture the event rather than merely report it. His newspaper chain perfected yellow journalism — the sensational, the moral panic engineered for circulation — and the line attributed to him, you furnish the pictures and I’ll furnish the war, is the whole method in nine words, whether or not he said it. Hearst is the dark twin of the two Pittsburgh papers from the Letters strand: the same press, the same power to set what a public believes, pointed not at building an academy but at manufacturing reality for power and profit. And the corporate fiction outlived the man — the Hearst Corporation remains one of the largest privately held media companies on earth. Now the careful part, because here documented history and persistent legend diverge, and the divergence is itself the subject. The documented fact: in the 1930s Hearst’s papers ran sensational anti-marijuana coverage that fed the moral panic behind the 1937 Marihuana Tax Act. News as propaganda — solid, and it is the node. The legend is larger and shakier: that Hearst drove prohibition specifically to protect his timber and paper holdings from hemp, a claim that traces overwhelmingly to one source, Jack Herer’s 1985 hemp-movement classic The Emperor Wears No Clothes, hugely influential and almost entirely unproven. And the related belief that Hearst made the film Reefer Madness is simply wrong: that film began in 1936 as Tell Your Children, produced by the independent George Hirliman and financed by a church group, then bought and recut into lurid exploitation by the showman Dwain Esper, who gave it the title it is remembered by — fueled downstream by the very cultural hysteria Hearst’s apparatus made viable.

The legend belongs in the piece rather than being cut from it, for the sharpest reason: the man who taught the press to manufacture reality became, after his death, the subject of a manufactured reality — a tidy conspiracy in which he is the puppet-master of prohibition, believed by millions, sourced to a single passionate book. To write the Hearst node honestly is to perform his own method on his own myth. Here is the documented panic; here is the folklore that grew on top of it; here is the seam between them. Read the picture, not the caption. Hearst is the caption machine, so the honest move is to show the reader how to read past him — including past the caption that later generations wrote about Hearst himself.

### Kristallnacht: the flare aimed at the builders

And here the strand must stop and look at the fire it has been circling, because the people who built this machine — the immigrant Jews of Tin Pan Alley and the studios and the Brill Building, the locked-out who walked into the new industries and made the living-asset machine of the century — are the people a fire was lit against. On the night of the ninth and tenth of November, 1938, across Germany and Austria, synagogues burned, Jewish shops were smashed, books were thrown onto pyres: Kristallnacht, the Night of Broken Glass, the flare that announced the catastrophe to come. The same decade that the excluded were colonizing the American imagination from Hollywood, the country many of them or their families had fled was burning the evidence that they had ever belonged. This is the fourth fire, and it is the one the strand cannot make a metaphor of, because it was literal and it was murder. The builders built a machine that manufactured an idealized America — and across the ocean the machinery of the state was manufacturing their erasure. Both are the twentieth century. The strand credits the makers; it owes them this, too: the flare was aimed at them, and it is part of why the machine they built carries the ache it does.

### Movement V — The Break and the Reset

All the way up the climb — silent picture to talkie to broadcast — the word stayed the engine. The screenplay, the lyric, the news copy, the ad script, the host’s patter: the image moved, but the word drove. The first real break came when the image began to lead and the word to follow. You can see it forming in the early cel animation projected behind a band like Pink Floyd, the visual no longer illustrating the song but competing with it, and it broke fully open with MTV in 1981, where the music video made the image the primary text and the song its soundtrack. This is the genuine hinge of the climb: the moment, after nearly a century, when the word stopped being the engine of the moving picture. The eye took the wheel from the ear.

Then the asset’s great strength became its mortal weakness, all at once, in the one industry where it had been strongest. The song was the original living asset — it earned by being copied — and for a century the music business controlled the copying. Reproducibility was the labels’ weapon. Then, in 1999, the substrate went weightless. Napster let anyone copy any song perfectly and instantly, for free, and the property that had made the song valuable became the thing that nearly destroyed the industry built on it. And here is where the strand stops being a story about music and becomes a story about everything, because what happened at the millennium was not a cycle. It is tempting to read it as one — the wheel of history, the eternal return, Alexandria burns and is rebuilt and burns again. It is not that. There is no recurrence here. The substrate changed in a way it had never changed in four thousand years, and the change does not loop back. It is a reset into the unknown — the Word, having climbed off the page at last, arriving in a condition the whole long timeline has no precedent for. What follows is not the old story repeating. It is the map running out.

### Movement VI — Who Owns the Master

The wall forced the question latent since Foster wrote his contracts by hand: when the catalogue is the asset, who owns the master? Not who wrote the song — who owns the recording, the matrix, the thing every copy is struck from. This is the slow war’s sharpest modern front, and it rhymes exactly with the Industry strand’s craft rail: Blake planing his own copper, Brett Weston burning his negatives so no one could print them after him — the maker fighting to control the matrix from which all copies descend. And note the timing, because it is not coincidence: the slow war’s bloodiest internal scene and its great external collapse arrive in the same decade, because they are the same pressure surfacing two ways. The asset became fiercely contested precisely as its scarcity was dissolving. The makers and the labels fought over the deed to a house the flood was already taking.

### Prince: Slave and Master, on a face

Prince fought Warner Bros. for ownership of his master recordings, and when he could not win it he did the thing that looks insane until you see it as the slow war made literal. In 1993 he changed his name to an unpronounceable symbol, and the following year began appearing in public with the word slave written on his face. His grievance was precise: Warner, he said, had taken the name his mother gave him, trademarked it, and used it as the marketing tool for all the music he wrote — the company owned “Prince” and everything sold under it, and he had become a pawn producing money for the label. Here the slow war drops every disguise and speaks its oldest pairing outright: Slave and Master, inked on a cheek, in the late twentieth century. The master is the means of production, and if you do not own it, it owns you — which is precisely what he said, in his own words: if you don’t own your masters, your master owns you. He severed the brand from the body; if the entity owned “Prince,” he would stop being Prince until he owned himself again. And it could only happen because the parallel germ line had been absorbed — he was fighting from inside the swallowed structure, a slave to a master held by a conglomerate that had swallowed the label that had swallowed his name. The resolution, when it came in 2014, ran through the very machinery that made the song property in the first place: a provision of copyright law that lets an author reclaim rights from a publisher after thirty-five years. With the deadline looming, leverage shifted to Prince, and the master reverted to the maker — but only after the flood, and only by the grace of the copyright that had captured it. The deed comes home, decades late, through the same door it left by.

### Taylor Swift: re-recording the matrix

And then the most public ownership fight of the present moment, fought by a maker who refused to wait thirty-five years for the law. When Taylor Swift’s master recordings were sold out from under her, she did the thing only the maker can do: she re-recorded her entire catalogue, song for song, as “Taylor’s Version,” manufacturing a new set of masters she owns and steering the world’s listening toward them, devaluing the originals she was denied. It is Brett Weston burning the negative, inverted into the streaming age — instead of destroying the matrix so no one can copy it, she struck a new matrix so every future copy descends from her. The maker cannot always seize the master, but the maker can sometimes make a new one and render the old one worthless, and Swift proved it works at planetary scale. Prince and Swift are not celebrity anecdotes; they are the slow war’s emotional center — makers fighting, across the wall, for the thing Dunbar held by default and Foster died without. He owned his book because no one wanted it yet. They had to wage war to own their songs because everyone wanted them. The climb made the word valuable; the value lit the slow war; the slow war is over the master.

### Movement VII — The Anchor and the Landing

*“Literature is the work of fire: it consumes what it names, leaving only the trace of its passing.”*

> *— after Maurice Blanchot, The Work of Fire (1949)*

### Charles Harbutt: the camera that refused

Every strand in this project has its conscience — the maker who refuses to let the machine make him complicit. In the Industry strand it was the printmaker who sold his work by his own hand and kept the money. In the Media strand it is Charles Harbutt, the precise counter-pole to Hearst. Harbutt joined Magnum Photos in 1963 and twice served as its president through the 1970s. He spent twenty years covering the news, and in 1969 he conceived America in Crisis, a book and exhibition portraying a furious, fracturing nation amid the civil-rights and anti-war years. But his deepest subject became the photograph’s own claim to truth — the very anxiety Plato named at the front of this strand, that the fixed image lies in a way the living moment does not. In 1972 he stood before an audience and said the radical thing: photography is not art, not fiction, not fantasy, not even memory — it is a real event, light that bounced off something that existed, once, just then, no longer. And his working creed is the line this strand closes on: sometimes pictures contradict their captions, and he could only hope people would read the picture and not the caption. By 1981 his questions about the power of the image had eroded his belief in journalism itself, and he left Magnum for a quieter, more interior photography. The man who could have spent his life supplying the pictures for other people’s wars walked away from the press’s reality-machine rather than be its camera.

There is a story told about why — one I carry from outside the public record, and so I set it down as exactly that: an account, not a documented fact. The night before an event — New Haven, around 1970 — federal agents were said to have checked into a hotel, only to turn up the next day undercover in the crowd, dressed as the very agitators the cameras would blame. Harbutt, the story goes, saw both faces of the same men and would not make the picture — because to publish it as it would inevitably be captioned was to become the instrument that muddied the who-is-who, that made things worse, that let the manufactured event be sold as the real one. Whether or not the record can confirm the specifics, the refusal is the point, and it is the exact shape of the craft rail: the maker who declines to let his work be used as the propaganda instrument. Hearst manufactures the reality. Harbutt refuses to be its lens. Read the picture, not the caption — and if reading it honestly means not taking it at all, then do not take it.

### The landing: Colonel Sanders, Joe Isuzu, and the nerve to sell the mascot as real

Everything in this strand has rehearsed one maneuver in public, for a century, before the art market ever deployed it: the corporate fiction sold as a living thing. It started in the burned valley with Carnegie branding himself and Heinz inventing a number that meant nothing. Advertising is where it was practiced most openly, and two figures mark its outer limits. Colonel Sanders is a fictitious entity wearing a string tie. The man existed — but “the Colonel,” the white-suited mascot who sells the chicken, is a constructed character, a fiction given a face and a folksy voice and sold to the public as a kindly real person: the human brand built backward, not a star manufactured into a property but a property manufactured into a man. And here is the blade this whole project has been sharpening: until Banksy, no one had the nerve to sell the mascot as a literally real, living artist. Colonel Sanders sells you chicken and everyone gets the wink. Banksy sells you the authorship itself — the constructed identity offered as a true hand at the auction block, the mascot sold as real, the wink withheld. This is the work of fire run as a business, and it is John’s verse inverted and monetized: the Word was with the maker, then the Word was the maker, and then the name Banksy consumed the maker who wore it, burned the person away, and left only the trace — a signature on a stencil, worth more than any hand because there is no longer a hand to find. The author is ash; the brand is the asset; and the auction pays most for the part that was never there.

And Joe Isuzu is the tell. In the 1980s the Isuzu car company ran ads featuring a spokesman who lied outrageously and constantly — grinning, preposterous, the truth printed in small type beneath him as he spoke. Joe Isuzu was the liar who taught the consumer not to trust the spokesman, and therefore to transfer trust to the corporation standing behind him, the entity sober enough to print the correction. It is the most sophisticated version of the maneuver: the fiction openly confessing it is a fiction, precisely to make the corporate entity behind it seem, by contrast, the trustworthy thing. The mascot admits he lies so the company need not. That is the corporate fiction achieving self-awareness as a sales technique — the deep structure beneath every move where a constructed front lends its deniability to the real owner behind the curtain.

### Coda: The Library That Does Not Burn

Return, at the end, to Alexandria — because the strand has been walking toward the next one the whole way, and the reset into the unknown has a concrete shape, and it is not what the old story would predict. The first Library faded by fire, the myth of the gathered word consumed and lost. We have built the next one — the total library, every recording and image and text and film, the whole climbed Word — and the astonishing thing is that it does not burn. It cannot, because it is no longer material. It has been transferred out of the physical world and into the electronic one, where it sits intact and complete and — this is the inversion — withheld. The old Alexandria was a tragedy of loss: the knowledge destroyed. The new Alexandria is a tragedy of enclosure: the knowledge preserved, whole, and fenced. Not burned. Not available. The paywall, the license gate, the firewall, the region lock, the rights-held archive you can see the spine of but cannot open — walled off by the corporate persons who hold it, the e-rulers, the descendants of the named entity born in the burned valley, because to reach the knowledge freely would touch a property right.

And the enclosure is not even faithful preservation. The corporate person captures the library, milks it for whatever can be sold — the licensable image, the streamable catalogue, the metered article, the harvested data — and drops what will not monetize: the unprofitable trace left to link-rot, deprecate, and quietly vanish, lost a second way, not by fire but by abandonment. What pays is locked up; what does not pay is let go. Meanwhile the public library — the people’s Alexandria, Carnegie’s own twenty-five hundred libraries, the commons that once held the backup in many buildings and many hands — is downsized, defunded, and structurally unequipped for a critical collapse. So the redundancy is gone. The old world survived the burning of any single Library because the word lived in many copies; the transfer to the electronic world concentrated the whole of it behind one corporate lock at the very moment the public backup was hollowed out. We firewalled the Word, shifted the mechanism from ownership to continuous licensing, and dismantled the redundancy in the same generation, and built a civilization with one copy, and handed a corporate person the key, and closed the public reading room for lack of funds. No one knows what happens if the lock fails.

That is the reset into the unknown, and it is why this is not a wheel turning. A cycle would burn the Library and rebuild it and burn it again. This does not loop. The next Alexandria cannot burn the old way, because it is not material; and it will not rebuild the old way, because the redundancy that made rebuilding possible was deliberately let go. The structure does not return to its beginning. It transfers, concentrates, encloses, and grows brittle, and then sits there — complete, unreachable, and fragile — over an unknown. Blanchot’s work of fire, completed and inverted: the old fire consumed the thing and left the trace; the new fire preserves the thing and withholds it, keeps the trace and sells access to it, and lets burn only what will not sell. The name consumed the maker. The firewall consumes the commons.

So the strand lands where it was always headed. From Moses unable to speak the unutterable breath of the Law, through Plato’s warning that the page kills the living word, past the Library’s fade and the verse that turned the Word into the thing itself, the word climbed: off the page, into the serial and the song and the moving picture and the broadcast, and the human was manufactured into a tradable brand the whole way up. The fire ran the length of it — Alexandria, Pittsburgh in 1845, the fire next time the appropriated carried onto the machine’s own channel, the flare aimed at the builders in 1938. The image peeled off the word at MTV; the substrate went weightless at Napster; the makers fought, win or lose, the slow war over the master — Foster dead with thirty-eight cents, Dunbar in his elevator, the Shirelles singing a song they did not own, Gordy building his own plant, Prince with slave on his cheek, Swift re-recording the matrix. And at the end stand two things at once: Banksy, the mascot sold as a real artist, the author consumed by the name and the auction paying most for the part that was never there; and behind him the locked Library, the whole climbed Word transferred, fenced, milked, and let to rot at the edges, while the public reading room goes dark. The voice that learned to sell learned, at the last, to sell its own author as a fiction and to fence the gathered word behind a corporate lock — and we do not know what happens next, because nothing on the four-thousand-year line has happened here before.

Charles Harbutt would have known what to do with it. The caption says a hand made this. The caption says the library is safe. Read the picture.

Hand-off. The Media strand ends where Economy–Markets begins: the living asset and the trophy object meet on a balance sheet, and the bet-against-motion gets priced at the hammer. That is where the auction forensics live — the May 2026 dual Banksy sales, the Bonhams pegging-event that failed on its own terms, the Brancusi estate-edition reopening, the living-asset-beats-trophy thesis. The voice that learned to sell, totted up at the rostrum.
